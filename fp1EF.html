Daily station readings

  1. This site has daily readings from weather stations around the United States. Each state has at least one station, with most states in the Midwest having 1-2. The source is the National Center for Environmental Information.

  2. Data description: variables include air temperature, precipitation, global solar radiation, relative humidity, surface infrared temperature, soil moisture and soil temperature data. Each state in the Midwest has 1-2 stations. Data is loaded by station and year, with data available dating back to 2000. The data below is just loaded for the MN Sandstone station for 2022.

  3. Data limitations: The data is fairly clean and recorded at regularly intervals. However, it would be nice if there was data dating back prior to 2000 and if there were more stations in the Midwest and/or Minnesota specifically so we could better understand climate change patterns related to urban areas and/or the Great Lakes. The two MN stations are both far north of the Twin Cities.

  4. Potential useful/interesting variables include temperature daily average, soil moisture 50 daily, station latitude and longitude, and precipitation daily calculation.

  1. Visualization of monthly precipitation in 2022.
sandstone2022 <- sandstone2022 %>%
  mutate(year = str_sub(LST_DATE, 1, 4), 
         month = str_sub(LST_DATE, 5, 6),
         day = str_sub(LST_DATE, 7,8), 
         date = as.Date(paste(year,month,day,sep="-"),"%Y-%m-%d"))

sandstone2022 %>%
  mutate(P_DAILY_CALC = as.numeric(P_DAILY_CALC), 
         P_DAILY_CALC = case_when(P_DAILY_CALC < 0 ~ 0, 
                                  TRUE ~ P_DAILY_CALC)) %>%
  group_by(month) %>%
  summarize(total_precipitation = sum(P_DAILY_CALC, na.rm=TRUE)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=month, y=total_precipitation))+
  geom_col(fill = "cadetblue4")+
  theme_classic()+
  labs(title = "Total precipitation in Sandstone, MN by month in 2022", y = "Precipitation (mm)", x = "Month")+
  theme(plot.title.position = "plot")

Ice coverage

  1. This site has multiple data sources on the Great Lakes. The ice coverage data are from the Canadian Ice Service and National Ice Center and are based on various imagery sources with resolutions down to 50 meters per pixel.

  2. While data can be downloaded for each of the Great Lakes, this specific dataset below just looks at Lake Superior percent ice coverage dating back to 1972 (through 2016). There appears to be one entry per month. This website has other data available on the Great Lakes, which includes water level data, hydrological input/output data, water temperature and cloud coverage.

  3. Data limitations: While the data goes pretty far back, we do not know exactly what part of Lake Superior is covered in ice. We just have one reading per month for the lake as a whole.

  4. This data on ice coverage just has two variables: date and ice coverage. If we choose to use this data later on, we look at the coverage for each lake.

# max ice coverage for lake superior
superior_ice <- read_csv("GLDData/data/ice/superiorMaxIcePerMonth.csv") %>%
  select(1:2)
colnames(superior_ice) <- c("Date", "PercentIce")

names(superior_ice)
## [1] "Date"       "PercentIce"
  1. Visualization of maximum ice coverage by year of Lake Superior
superior_ice %>%
  mutate(Date= as.Date(Date, format = "%m/%d/%Y"), 
         year = as.numeric(format(Date, "%Y"))) %>%
  group_by(year) %>%
  summarize(max_coverage = max(PercentIce)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=year, y=max_coverage))+
  geom_point()+
  geom_smooth(se=FALSE)+
  theme_classic()+
  labs(title = "Maximum Ice Coverage of Lake Superior by Year", y = "Percent Ice Coverage (%)", x = "Year")+
  theme(plot.title.position = "plot")

Limitations with that data may include that many stations only have one sample date, and comparing values of that chemical across different stations at different dates may not be a fair analysis.
